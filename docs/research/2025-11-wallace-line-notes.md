## Overview
The Wallace Line is a major biogeographical boundary running between Bali and Lombok, and between Borneo and Sulawesi. It separates Asian fauna from Australasian fauna. It is one of the most fascinating natural boundaries on Earth, because the distance between islands can be as little as 35 km yet species differ dramatically.

This boundary reflects millions of years of plate tectonics, sea depth, and isolated evolutionary trajectories.

The Weber Line and Lydekker Line are additional, more precise boundaries used by biogeographers to describe faunal gradients across the Indonesian archipelago.

---

## 1. Wallace Line — Summary
- Proposed by Alfred Russel Wallace (co-discoverer of evolution by natural selection).  
- Runs between Bali → Lombok and Borneo → Sulawesi.  
- Marks the transition from **Asian fauna** (tigers, orangutans, elephants) to **Australasian fauna** (marsupials, cockatoos, cuscus).  
- Even today, despite human movement and trade, species distribution still reflects this boundary.  

**Cause:**  
Deep ocean trenches that never formed land bridges during the Ice Ages, preventing animals from crossing.

---

## 2. Weber Line — Summary
- Drawn by Max Weber (Dutch zoologist).  
- Represents the **point of equilibrium** where Asian and Australasian species are approximately equal in number.  
- Sits east of the Wallace Line.  
- Often considered a more biologically accurate faunal divide, but more complex.

---

## 3. Lydekker Line — Summary
- Proposed by Richard Lydekker.  
- Marks the **western boundary of the Australasian continental shelf (Sahul Shelf)**.  
- Runs along the western edge of New Guinea and Australia’s continental influence.  
- Lies further east than both Wallace and Weber lines.  

---

## 4. Why These Lines Matter
- They help explain evolution, biodiversity patterns, and species isolation.  
- They showcase how geography shapes life on Earth.  
- They show Indonesia as one of the most biologically complex regions on the planet.  
- They offer fascinating storytelling opportunities for education, tourism, nature content, and creative science communication.
